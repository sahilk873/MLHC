\begin{abstract}
Pulse oximetry bias and hidden hypoxemia have been documented across race/ethnicity groups, but it is unclear whether race-stratified debiasing improves outcomes when validated against measured skin tone. We develop debiasing and calibration strategies on the multi-center BOLD dataset (49,093 paired measurements) and externally validate on ENCoDE (617 paired measurements), which provides prospectively measured skin tone (Monk scale) across devices and locations. We evaluate continuous error and hidden hypoxemia, report bootstrap confidence intervals, and analyze worst-group disparities across race/ethnicity and skin tone bins. On BOLD, simple models improve overall MAE versus the baseline (SpO\textsubscript{2} as SaO\textsubscript{2} proxy) and reduce worst-group gaps. On ENCoDE, transfer evaluation yields wide CIs and no clear, robust evidence that race-stratified gains in BOLD translate cleanly to Monk-bin improvements, emphasizing the need for external validation on measured pigmentation. We provide a script-driven pipeline that generates all tables and figures from code; results are reproducible given access to the source datasets.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Pulse oximetry is widely used to monitor oxygen saturation in clinical care. Multiple studies report that SpO\textsubscript{2} can overestimate SaO\textsubscript{2} among darker-skinned patients, leading to hidden hypoxemia and potential delays in care.
As machine learning models and calibration strategies are proposed to correct these biases, it remains unclear whether improvements measured across race/ethnicity actually translate to improvements across measured skin tone \cite{sjoding2020racial,wong2021discrepancies,encode2024}.

In this work, we quantify bias in the BOLD dataset, train debiasing models on BOLD, and externally validate these models on ENCoDE, which includes prospectively measured skin tone (Monk scale) across devices and locations. We emphasize rigorous evaluation with bootstrap confidence intervals, worst-group metrics, and sensitivity analyses across hidden hypoxemia thresholds. Our main contribution is an external validation framework that tests whether race-stratified gains on retrospective ICU data appear to generalize to measured skin tone under realistic data constraints.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
\item Quantify pulse oximetry bias and hidden hypoxemia on BOLD with bootstrap CIs and worst-group metrics.
\item Train and analyze multiple simple debiasing/calibration models, emphasizing the MAE--hidden-hypoxemia tradeoff.
\item Perform external validation on ENCoDE with measured skin tone, showing when race-stratified gains do and do not generalize.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Generalizable Insights about Machine Learning in the Context of Healthcare}
\begin{itemize}
\item Avoid evaluating fairness only on training-domain demographic proxies; include measured causal attributes (e.g., skin tone) when feasible.
\item Treat calibration and MAE as secondary to clinically weighted errors for safety-critical endpoints (e.g., hidden hypoxemia).
\item Design evaluation to stress-test deployment shift (devices, locations, sites) rather than relying on in-domain validation only.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

Prior work documents pulse oximetry bias and hidden hypoxemia across racial groups \cite{sjoding2020racial,wong2021discrepancies}. Recent efforts explore calibration and debiasing strategies for physiological measurements and downstream risk models \cite{zadrozny2002,obermeyer2019}. Broader clinical-ML fairness work emphasizes the limitations of race/ethnicity-based corrections and measurement choices \cite{vyas2020hidden,suresh2021framework}, and highlights that subgroup performance can shift under deployment changes such as device or site variation \cite{suresh2021framework}. Our work differs by explicitly validating fairness conclusions against measured skin tone, using ENCoDE's prospectively collected Monk scale metrics, and by focusing on the generalization of debiasing methods across datasets and device contexts.

Empirically, our findings align with prior work in direction (systematic overestimation in darker-skinned groups and non-trivial hidden hypoxemia gaps), but the magnitude and stability of gains differ by dataset, device mix, and event rates. Compared to earlier single-dataset studies, we highlight that debiasing gains measured on retrospective ICU cohorts can attenuate when evaluated on prospectively measured skin tone, reinforcing that study design and measurement choices (race vs. pigmentation) substantially influence observed fairness outcomes.

\section{Methods}

\textbf{Endpoints and clinical thresholds.} We compute continuous error $e = \text{SaO}_2 - \text{SpO}_2$, absolute error $|e|$, and hidden hypoxemia defined as $\text{SaO}_2 < 88$ with $\text{SpO}_2 \geq T$ for thresholds $T \in \{90,92,94\}$ (primary $T=92$), consistent with prior clinical definitions of occult hypoxemia and common SpO\textsubscript{2} alert thresholds in the pulse-oximetry bias literature \cite{sjoding2020racial,wong2021discrepancies}. We also compute calibration curves for SaO\textsubscript{2} vs. SpO\textsubscript{2}.

\textbf{Skin tone measurement and binning.} ENCoDE provides prospectively administered Monk Skin Tone Scale measurements across anatomical locations and devices. We extract Monk values from temporary concept names and numeric measurement values, then aggregate per person using the median across available locations. Primary bins are light (1--3), medium (4--6), and dark (7--10); location-specific Monk values are reported in sensitivity analyses. Missing Monk values are retained for overall metrics but excluded from skin tone stratification.

\textbf{Race vs.\ skin tone framing.} Race/ethnicity is a social construct that proxies multiple social, structural, and historical factors; skin tone is a proximate optical determinant for oximetry bias. We therefore treat subgroup differences as associational and do not interpret race-stratified improvements as causal or mechanistic.

\textbf{ENCoDE pairing algorithm.} For each SaO\textsubscript{2} measurement, we identify candidate SpO\textsubscript{2} measurements within the same person and visit, occurring at or before the SaO\textsubscript{2} time. We keep candidates within a 0--5 minute window and select the nearest preceding SpO\textsubscript{2} (tie-breaking by the latest timestamp within the window). We then keep one pair per visit by selecting the earliest paired SaO\textsubscript{2} within each visit. Multiple visits per patient are retained, but train/test splits are performed at the patient (or hospitalization) level to prevent leakage. ENCoDE is used only for external evaluation and is never used for model tuning.

\textbf{Debiasing models and objectives.} We evaluate: (1) baseline $\hat{\text{SaO}}_2 = \text{SpO}_2$, (2) ridge regression using SpO\textsubscript{2} only with loss $\sum_i (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2 + \alpha \lVert w \rVert_2^2$ ($\alpha=1.0$ fixed; intercept included), (3) reweighted ridge with loss $\sum_i \omega_{g(i)} (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2 + \alpha \lVert w \rVert_2^2$ where $\omega_{g}$ is inverse race/ethnicity frequency in BOLD (weights fixed), (4) isotonic regression calibration constrained to be monotone in SpO\textsubscript{2}, and (5) safe offset models $\hat{\text{SaO}}_2 = f(\text{SpO}_2) - \delta$ with $\delta \ge 0$ tuned on BOLD training data only to reduce hypoxemia-miss FNR for the predicted SaO\textsubscript{2} threshold at 88\% (target $\le 0.5$, offsets 0--5 in 0.1 increments). This target is exploratory and not clinically endorsed; safe offsets are included as a tradeoff illustration and should not be interpreted as deployment-ready corrections. For transfer evaluation, we restrict to the feature set available in both datasets (SpO\textsubscript{2} only).

\textbf{Splits, tuning, and bootstrap protocol.} Primary tables and figures use a canonical 80/20 split on BOLD at the patient (or hospitalization) level when available (seed 1337); repeated splits over seeds $\{7,11,19,29,43\}$ are summarized in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:repeated-splits}. Hyperparameters and offsets are fixed or tuned on BOLD training data only. ENCoDE is an external test set and is not used for tuning or model selection. The unit of analysis is the paired SaO\textsubscript{2}--SpO\textsubscript{2} measurement; race and Monk bins are person-level attributes inherited by each pair. We use a cluster bootstrap: sample patient (or hospitalization) IDs with replacement, then include all pairs from sampled clusters. If a patient has multiple visits, all of their visits/pairs enter the resample when that patient is drawn. Uncertainty is estimated via 1,000 bootstrap resamples on fixed test sets; we report percentile 95\% CIs. Calibration curves use 10 quantile bins of predicted SaO\textsubscript{2}.

\textbf{Fairness metrics (definitions).} For each pair $i$, define the hidden hypoxemia label $h_i(T) = \mathbb{1}[\text{SaO}_2 < 88 \land \text{SpO}_2 \ge T]$. For FNR/FPR, define the hypoxemia label $y_i=\mathbb{1}[\text{SaO}_2 < 88]$ and prediction $\hat{y}_i=\mathbb{1}[\hat{\text{SaO}}_2 < 88]$. Grouped FNR is $\text{FNR}_g = \Pr(\hat{y}=0 \mid y=1, g)$; grouped FPR is $\text{FPR}_g = \Pr(\hat{y}=1 \mid y=0, g)$. Gaps are defined as $\max_g \text{metric}_g - \min_g \text{metric}_g$ for MAE, FNR, and FPR (and for hidden hypoxemia rates when reported). Worst-group performance is the maximum group-specific metric (e.g., $\max_g \text{MAE}_g$). Groups with $n<30$ are excluded from gap calculations. For FNR/FPR, groups with zero events in the conditioning set (no $y=1$ for FNR or no $y=0$ for FPR) yield undefined rates and are excluded from gap calculations; all such groups are still reported descriptively in tables.

\section{Cohort}

\subsection{Cohort Selection}
BOLD is a multi-center ICU dataset with paired ABG and pulse oximetry measurements \cite{bold2023physionet}. ENCoDE is a prospective dataset with OMOP tables and measured skin tone (Monk scale) across multiple devices and anatomical locations \cite{encode2024}. We include visits with at least one SaO\textsubscript{2} measurement and a preceding SpO\textsubscript{2} within 0--5 minutes in the same visit, exclude missing timestamps and implausible oxygen saturation values, and select the first valid pair per hospitalization/visit to reduce dependence. The primary analysis is at the pair level (one SaO\textsubscript{2}--SpO\textsubscript{2} pair per visit/hospitalization), and multiple visits per patient are handled by selecting a single pair per visit and splitting at the patient/hospitalization level to avoid leakage. Table~\ref{tab:cohort} summarizes cohort sizes (BOLD $n=49{,}093$, ENCoDE $n=617$) and group distributions. The canonical BOLD split yields 39,262 train pairs and 9,831 test pairs; hidden hypoxemia event counts are 1,487/1,183/862 for $T=90/92/94$. ENCoDE has 2 hidden hypoxemia events at each threshold (2/2/2 for $T=90/92/94$). Several subgroups are small (e.g., BOLD Native Hawaiian/Pacific Islander $n=9$, More Than One Race $n=3$; ENCoDE light skin tone $n=25$), which we explicitly report and interpret with caution.

\subsection{BOLD Pairing and Preprocessing}
BOLD provides paired SaO\textsubscript{2}--SpO\textsubscript{2} measurements; we use the provided pairs and do not reconstruct timing-based pairs. We exclude pairs with missing timestamps or oxygen saturation values and remove implausible values outside 0--100. When multiple pairs exist per hospitalization, we retain the first valid pair to reduce dependence.

\subsection{Data Extraction}
We auto-discover raw files, generate a manifest with hashes, and load tables with explicit schema validation. We drop rows with missing SaO\textsubscript{2} or SpO\textsubscript{2} and exclude implausible oxygen saturation values outside 0--100. For ENCoDE, we parse temporary concept IDs to extract skin tone and image-derived features, and index available images by device and location.

\subsection{Feature Choices}
Features include SpO\textsubscript{2} and demographics (age, sex, race/ethnicity where available) for cohort description and subgrouping. Model training uses SpO\textsubscript{2} only to ensure feature alignment across datasets; skin tone is computed from Monk scale values and binned into light/medium/dark groups. We standardize feature names across datasets to enable transfer evaluation.

\subsection{Reproducibility}
All results are generated by scripts from a clean checkout with fixed seeds (default seed 1337; repeated splits use $\{7,11,19,29,43\}$), explicit manifests, and environment specifications in \texttt{pyproject.toml} and \texttt{REPRODUCE.md}. We will release the full code and scripts upon publication; data access follows PhysioNet terms for BOLD and ENCoDE.

\section{Results on Real Data}

\subsection{Evaluation Approach/Study Design}
We train debiasing models on BOLD and evaluate on the held-out BOLD test split (canonical seed 1337); repeated split results are summarized in Appendix Table~\ref{tab:repeated-splits}. We then perform external validation on ENCoDE using transferable feature sets only. ENCoDE is never used for tuning or model selection. All metrics include patient-clustered bootstrap confidence intervals computed on fixed test sets, and we report worst-group performance across race/ethnicity (BOLD, ENCoDE) and skin tone bins (ENCoDE).

\subsection{Key Results}
On BOLD, ridge regression reduces MAE from 2.456 (95\% CI 2.409–2.501) to 2.213 (2.175–2.253), and reweighted ridge achieves 2.251 (2.213–2.296). The race/ethnicity MAE gap decreases from 0.838 (0.633–1.255) at baseline to 0.741 (0.500–1.075) for ridge, while the hidden hypoxemia FNR gap decreases from 0.316 (0.146–0.917) to 0.123 (0.090–0.194) for ridge and 0.053 (0.047–0.095) for reweighted ridge.

On ENCoDE, ridge reduces MAE from 2.484 (2.217–2.678) to 1.344 (1.146–1.482); the skin tone MAE gap CIs remain wide (e.g., 0.004–0.429 for ridge), and hidden hypoxemia FNR gap CIs span 0–1.0, reflecting limited power for subgroup event rates. With patient-level clustered resampling, low event counts in some skin tone bins yield extremely wide CIs, as intended to reflect uncertainty.

\subsection{Baseline Disparities}
Figure~\ref{fig:bold-error-race} shows BOLD error distributions by race/ethnicity, and Figure~\ref{fig:encode-error-skintone} shows ENCoDE error by skin tone bin. Figure~\ref{fig:bold-race-dist} reports BOLD race/ethnicity distribution, and Figure~\ref{fig:encode-skintone-dist} reports ENCoDE Monk-bin distribution. In BOLD, the error distribution for Black patients is shifted upward relative to White patients, indicating higher overestimation on average. In ENCoDE, darker Monk bins show higher median error than lighter bins, even though uncertainty is large due to small subgroup sizes. Subgroup sizes and hidden hypoxemia event counts are reported in Table~\ref{tab:group-counts}; for example, ENCoDE light skin tone has $n=25$ with 0 hidden hypoxemia events at $T=92$, while ENCoDE dark has $n=264$ with 2 events, leading to wide CIs for subgroup-specific FNR.

\subsection{External Validation on ENCoDE}
Table~\ref{tab:encode-models} shows external validation on ENCoDE using calibration-only debiasing (SpO\textsubscript{2}-only models). Transfer models improve overall MAE in ENCoDE compared with the baseline; however, worst-bin skin tone gaps have wide confidence intervals due to low event counts. Table~\ref{tab:encode-race-vs-skintone-main} directly compares race- vs Monk-bin MAE/FNR/FPR gaps for baseline and ridge; CIs are wide and often have near-zero lower bounds. For example, while ridge reduces the BOLD race MAE gap from 0.838 to 0.741, the ENCoDE race MAE gap bootstrap mean is 0.226 (95\% CI 0.014--0.539) and the Monk-bin MAE gap bootstrap mean is 0.170 (95\% CI 0.004--0.429), reflecting wide uncertainty. We therefore do not find clear, robust evidence that race-based gains in BOLD translate cleanly to Monk-bin improvements in ENCoDE. We further observe that gains measured across race/ethnicity in BOLD do not always translate to improvements across measured skin tone in ENCoDE, consistent with differences in device mix and anatomical measurement location (Appendix Figures~\ref{fig:encode-device}--\ref{fig:encode-location}). Device and location sensitivity analyses show consistent shifts in MAE across hardware and sites, suggesting measurement context contributes to transfer variability.

\section{Discussion}

We find that debiasing on retrospective ICU data reduces overall error and can reduce worst-group disparities; however, external validation on ENCoDE provides limited and uncertain evidence about whether race/ethnicity-based gains translate to measured skin tone improvements, due to small $n$ and wide CIs. Table~\ref{tab:encode-race-vs-skintone-main} summarizes this comparison for MAE/FNR/FPR gaps and shows substantial uncertainty rather than definitive failure of transfer. This supports the need for measured pigmentation in fairness evaluation and suggests that race/ethnicity proxies are insufficient for clinical deployment claims. The tradeoff between overall MAE and hidden hypoxemia highlights the importance of explicitly prioritizing clinically important errors. For example, in BOLD the ridge model reduces MAE from 2.456 to 2.213 while reducing the FNR gap from 0.316 to 0.123, whereas safe-offset models reduce missed hypoxemia rates (0.034 to 0.021) at the cost of substantially higher MAE (4.218), a tradeoff that may be clinically unacceptable depending on deployment goals.

We interpret the partial transfer of race/ethnicity-based gains as consistent with cohort and measurement differences: ENCoDE includes multiple devices and anatomical locations, and the device/location sensitivity analyses show non-trivial variation in MAE. In addition, ENCoDE subgroup sizes and event counts are smaller than BOLD (Table~\ref{tab:group-counts}), increasing uncertainty and making some fairness estimates unstable. Mechanistically, race/ethnicity proxies multiple constructs (access, SES, geography), whereas skin tone is closer to the optical mechanism of oximeter bias, so divergence is plausible. These factors emphasize that fairness conclusions are sensitive to device mix, measurement location, and cohort composition.

We draw three explicit takeaways for MLHC fairness practice: (1) validate debiasing claims on measured clinical attributes (e.g., skin tone) whenever available rather than relying solely on race/ethnicity, (2) report tradeoffs between aggregate error and clinically critical errors like hidden hypoxemia, and (3) accompany subgroup metrics with group counts and event rates to avoid over-interpreting unstable estimates.

\paragraph{Limitations}
ENCoDE has relatively low event rates for hidden hypoxemia, yielding wide bootstrap CIs and limited power for subgroup FNR/FPR estimates. Both datasets are drawn from specific institutions and device mixes, so findings may not generalize to other care settings. Transfer models are constrained by feature overlap between datasets; SpO\textsubscript{2}-only models likely underestimate what richer covariates could achieve. We flag models as pathological when test-set FNR is $\geq 0.99$ and report them in the appendix; isotonic variants met this criterion in some splits and are excluded from the main results. Finally, we do not analyze downstream clinical outcomes; our focus is measurement bias rather than clinical decision impact.

\paragraph{Ethics and Data Use}
This study uses de-identified, publicly available datasets (BOLD and ENCoDE) accessed under their respective data use agreements. We report only aggregate results and do not attempt re-identification. No additional patient contact or intervention was performed. Miscalibrated corrections could worsen disparities if deployed without measured skin tone or local validation; our results should not be used for deployment decisions without site-specific evaluation. Finally, focusing on measurement error alone may miss downstream clinical harms or benefits, underscoring the need for outcome-level assessment in future work.
