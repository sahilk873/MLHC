\begin{abstract}
Pulse oximetry bias and hidden hypoxemia have been documented across race/ethnicity, but it is unclear whether race-stratified debiasing improves outcomes when validated against measured skin tone. We develop debiasing and calibration strategies on the multi-center BOLD dataset and externally validate on ENCoDE, which provides prospectively measured skin tone (Monk scale) across devices and locations. We evaluate continuous error and hidden hypoxemia, report bootstrap confidence intervals, and analyze worst-group disparities across race/ethnicity and skin tone bins. On BOLD, simple models improve overall MAE versus the baseline (SpO\textsubscript{2} as SaO\textsubscript{2} proxy) and reduce worst-group gaps. On ENCoDE, transfer evaluation shows that race-based gains do not always translate to improved performance across measured skin tone, highlighting the need for validation on measured pigmentation. Our pipeline is fully reproducible and generates all tables and figures from script.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Pulse oximetry is widely used to monitor oxygen saturation in clinical care. Multiple studies report that SpO\textsubscript{2} can overestimate SaO\textsubscript{2} among darker-skinned patients, leading to hidden hypoxemia and potential delays in care. As machine learning models and calibration strategies are proposed to correct these biases, it remains unclear whether improvements measured across race/ethnicity actually translate to improvements across measured skin tone\cite{pulseox-bias,hidden-hypoxemia,skin-tone-metrics}.

In this work, we quantify bias in the BOLD dataset, train debiasing models on BOLD, and externally validate these models on ENCoDE, which includes prospectively measured skin tone (Monk scale) across devices and locations. We emphasize rigorous evaluation with bootstrap confidence intervals, worst-group metrics, and sensitivity analyses across hidden hypoxemia thresholds. Our main contribution is an external validation showing when race-stratified gains on retrospective ICU data generalize---or fail to generalize---to measured skin tone.

\subsection*{Generalizable Insights about Machine Learning in the Context of Healthcare}
\begin{itemize}
\item Demographic proxies (race/ethnicity) can be insufficient for fairness claims; validation with measured clinical attributes (e.g., skin tone) can change conclusions.
\item Debiasing methods may reduce overall error while worsening rare but clinically important errors (hidden hypoxemia), motivating explicit tradeoff analysis.
\item External validation on prospectively measured data is essential for claims of fairness in clinical ML models.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

Prior work documents pulse oximetry bias and hidden hypoxemia across racial groups\cite{pulseox-bias,hidden-hypoxemia}. Recent efforts explore calibration and debiasing strategies for physiological measurements and downstream risk models\cite{calibration-clinical,ml-fairness-health}. Our work differs by explicitly validating fairness conclusions against measured skin tone, using ENCoDE's prospectively collected Monk scale metrics, and by focusing on the generalization of debiasing methods across datasets and device contexts.

\section{Methods}

\textbf{Endpoints.} We compute continuous error $e = \text{SaO}_2 - \text{SpO}_2$, absolute error $|e|$, and hidden hypoxemia defined as $\text{SaO}_2 < 88$ with $\text{SpO}_2 \geq T$ for thresholds $T \in \{90,92,94\}$ (primary $T=92$). We also compute calibration curves for SaO\textsubscript{2} vs. SpO\textsubscript{2}.

\textbf{Debiasing models.} We evaluate: (1) baseline $\hat{\text{SaO}}_2 = \text{SpO}_2$, (2) ridge regression using SpO\textsubscript{2} and available covariates, (3) reweighted ridge to reduce worst-group error, (4) isotonic regression calibration, and (5) safe offset models that prioritize reduction of hidden hypoxemia at the cost of MAE. For transfer evaluation, we train reduced-feature variants when ENCoDE lacks a covariate.\n
\textbf{Evaluation.} We report MAE, RMSE, hidden hypoxemia FNR/FPR, and worst-group performance. Uncertainty is estimated via 1,000 bootstrap resamples. To assess robustness, we perform repeated train/test splits with patient-level separation and summarize variability across seeds.

\section{Cohort}

\subsection{Cohort Selection}
BOLD is a multi-center ICU dataset with paired ABG and pulse oximetry measurements. ENCoDE is a prospective dataset with OMOP tables and measured skin tone (Monk scale) across multiple devices and anatomical locations. We apply strict pairing logic to identify ABG SaO\textsubscript{2} measurements and the nearest preceding SpO\textsubscript{2} within 5 minutes in the same visit.

\subsection{Data Extraction}
We auto-discover raw files, generate a manifest with hashes, and load tables with explicit schema validation. For ENCoDE, we parse temporary concept IDs to extract skin tone and image-derived features, and index available images by device and location.

\subsection{Feature Choices}
Features include SpO\textsubscript{2}, demographics (age, sex, race/ethnicity where available), and available covariates. Skin tone is computed from Monk scale values and binned into light/medium/dark groups. We standardize feature names across datasets to enable transfer evaluation.

\section{Required: Discussion}

We find that debiasing on retrospective ICU data reduces overall error and can reduce worst-group disparities; however, external validation on ENCoDE reveals that race-based gains do not uniformly translate to measured skin tone improvements. This supports the need for measured pigmentation in fairness evaluation, and suggests that race-ethnicity proxies may be insufficient for clinical deployment claims. The tradeoff between overall MAE and hidden hypoxemia highlights the importance of explicitly prioritizing clinically important errors.

\paragraph{Limitations}
ENCoDE has relatively low event rates for hidden hypoxemia, limiting power for some subgroup estimates. Transfer models are constrained by feature overlap between datasets. Our isotonic calibration variants occasionally exhibited pathological increases in hidden hypoxemia and were excluded from the main analysis. Finally, we do not analyze downstream clinical outcomes; our focus is measurement bias rather than clinical decision impact.

\subsection{Isotonic Calibration Pathology}
In some splits, isotonic regression reduced MAE while substantially increasing hidden hypoxemia rates. We exclude these models from the main results and report them here to illustrate the importance of monitoring clinically meaningful error types alongside aggregate metrics.
