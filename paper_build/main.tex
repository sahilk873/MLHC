\begin{abstract}
Pulse oximetry bias and hidden hypoxemia have been documented across race/ethnicity, but it is unclear whether race-stratified debiasing improves outcomes when validated against measured skin tone. We develop debiasing and calibration strategies on the multi-center BOLD dataset (49,093 paired measurements) and externally validate on ENCoDE (617 paired measurements), which provides prospectively measured skin tone (Monk scale) across devices and locations. We evaluate continuous error and hidden hypoxemia, report bootstrap confidence intervals, and analyze worst-group disparities across race/ethnicity and skin tone bins. On BOLD, simple models improve overall MAE versus the baseline (SpO\textsubscript{2} as SaO\textsubscript{2} proxy) and reduce worst-group gaps. On ENCoDE, transfer evaluation shows that race-based gains do not always translate to improved performance across measured skin tone, highlighting the need for validation on measured pigmentation. Our pipeline is fully reproducible and generates all tables and figures from script.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Pulse oximetry is widely used to monitor oxygen saturation in clinical care. Multiple studies report that SpO\textsubscript{2} can overestimate SaO\textsubscript{2} among darker-skinned patients, leading to hidden hypoxemia and potential delays in care. As machine learning models and calibration strategies are proposed to correct these biases, it remains unclear whether improvements measured across race/ethnicity actually translate to improvements across measured skin tone\cite{sjoding2020racial,wong2021discrepancies,encode2024}.

In this work, we quantify bias in the BOLD dataset, train debiasing models on BOLD, and externally validate these models on ENCoDE, which includes prospectively measured skin tone (Monk scale) across devices and locations. We emphasize rigorous evaluation with bootstrap confidence intervals, worst-group metrics, and sensitivity analyses across hidden hypoxemia thresholds. Our main contribution is an external validation showing when race-stratified gains on retrospective ICU data generalize---or fail to generalize---to measured skin tone.

\subsection*{Generalizable Insights about Machine Learning in the Context of Healthcare}
\begin{itemize}
\item Demographic proxies (race/ethnicity) can be insufficient for fairness claims; validation with measured clinical attributes (e.g., skin tone) can change conclusions.
\item Debiasing methods may reduce overall error while worsening rare but clinically important errors (hidden hypoxemia), motivating explicit tradeoff analysis.
\item External validation on prospectively measured data is essential for claims of fairness in clinical ML models.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

Prior work documents pulse oximetry bias and hidden hypoxemia across racial groups\cite{sjoding2020racial,wong2021discrepancies}. Recent efforts explore calibration and debiasing strategies for physiological measurements and downstream risk models\cite{zadrozny2002,obermeyer2019}. Our work differs by explicitly validating fairness conclusions against measured skin tone, using ENCoDE's prospectively collected Monk scale metrics, and by focusing on the generalization of debiasing methods across datasets and device contexts.

Empirically, our findings align with prior work in direction (systematic overestimation in darker-skinned groups and non-trivial hidden hypoxemia gaps), but the magnitude and stability of gains differ by dataset, device mix, and event rates. Compared to earlier single-dataset studies, we highlight that debiasing gains measured on retrospective ICU cohorts can attenuate when evaluated on prospectively measured skin tone, reinforcing that study design and measurement choices (race vs. pigmentation) substantially influence observed fairness outcomes.

\section{Methods}

\textbf{Endpoints.} We compute continuous error $e = \text{SaO}_2 - \text{SpO}_2$, absolute error $|e|$, and hidden hypoxemia defined as $\text{SaO}_2 < 88$ with $\text{SpO}_2 \geq T$ for thresholds $T \in \{90,92,94\}$ (primary $T=92$). We also compute calibration curves for SaO\textsubscript{2} vs. SpO\textsubscript{2}.

\textbf{Debiasing models.} We evaluate: (1) baseline $\hat{\text{SaO}}_2 = \text{SpO}_2$, (2) ridge regression using SpO\textsubscript{2} only (Ridge, $\alpha=1.0$), (3) reweighted ridge to reduce worst-group error, (4) isotonic regression calibration (SpO\textsubscript{2} only), and (5) safe offset models that prioritize reduction of hidden hypoxemia at the cost of MAE. For reweighting, we upweight under-represented race/ethnicity groups in BOLD using inverse group frequency and optimize a weighted loss to reduce worst-group MAE. Safe models add a non-negative offset to predictions; the offset is tuned on training data to reduce hidden hypoxemia FNR (target $\leq 0.5$; offsets searched from 0 to 5 in 0.1 increments). For transfer evaluation, we restrict to the feature set available in both datasets (SpO\textsubscript{2} only).\n

\textbf{Splits and hyperparameters.} We use an 80/20 train/test split at the patient level when IDs are available (patient or hospitalization identifiers), with fixed random seeds. Ridge hyperparameters are fixed ($\alpha=1.0$). Repeated splits (seeds $\{7,11,19,29,43\}$) are used to assess stability.\n
\textbf{Evaluation.} We report MAE, RMSE, hidden hypoxemia FNR/FPR, and worst-group performance. Uncertainty is estimated via 1,000 nonparametric bootstrap resamples clustered at the patient (or hospitalization) level when IDs are available. Group metrics exclude groups with $n<30$ from gap calculations; smaller groups are still reported descriptively. Calibration curves use 10 quantile bins of predicted SaO\textsubscript{2}.

\section{Cohort}

\subsection{Cohort Selection}
BOLD is a multi-center ICU dataset with paired ABG and pulse oximetry measurements\cite{bold2023physionet}. ENCoDE is a prospective dataset with OMOP tables and measured skin tone (Monk scale) across multiple devices and anatomical locations\cite{encode2024}. We include visits with at least one SaO\textsubscript{2} measurement and a preceding SpO\textsubscript{2} within 5 minutes in the same visit, exclude missing timestamps and implausible oxygen saturation values, and select the first valid pair per hospitalization/visit to reduce dependence. Table~\ref{tab:cohort} summarizes cohort sizes (BOLD $n=49{,}093$, ENCoDE $n=617$) and group distributions. Several subgroups are small (e.g., BOLD Native Hawaiian/Pacific Islander $n=9$, More Than One Race $n=3$; ENCoDE light skin tone $n=25$), which we explicitly report and interpret with caution.

\subsection{Data Extraction}
We auto-discover raw files, generate a manifest with hashes, and load tables with explicit schema validation. We drop rows with missing SaO\textsubscript{2} or SpO\textsubscript{2} and exclude implausible oxygen saturation values outside 0--100. For ENCoDE, we parse temporary concept IDs to extract skin tone and image-derived features, and index available images by device and location.

\subsection{Feature Choices}
Features include SpO\textsubscript{2} and demographics (age, sex, race/ethnicity where available) for cohort description and subgrouping. Model training uses SpO\textsubscript{2} only to ensure feature alignment across datasets; skin tone is computed from Monk scale values and binned into light/medium/dark groups. We standardize feature names across datasets to enable transfer evaluation.

\section{Results on Real Data}

\subsection{Evaluation Approach/Study Design}
We train debiasing models on BOLD and evaluate on held-out BOLD test splits. We then perform external validation on ENCoDE using transferable feature sets. All metrics include bootstrap confidence intervals, and we report worst-group performance across race/ethnicity (BOLD, ENCoDE) and skin tone bins (ENCoDE).

\subsection{Key Results}
On BOLD, ridge regression reduces MAE from 2.456 (95\% CI 2.409–2.501) to 2.213 (2.175–2.253), and reweighted ridge achieves 2.251 (2.213–2.296). The race/ethnicity MAE gap decreases from 0.838 (0.633–1.255) at baseline to 0.741 (0.500–1.075) for ridge, while the hidden hypoxemia FNR gap decreases from 0.316 (0.146–0.917) to 0.123 (0.090–0.194) for ridge and 0.053 (0.047–0.095) for reweighted ridge.\n
On ENCoDE, ridge reduces MAE from 2.484 (2.217–2.678) to 1.344 (1.146–1.482); the skin tone MAE gap CIs remain wide (e.g., 0.004–0.429 for ridge), and hidden hypoxemia FNR gap CIs span 0–1.0, reflecting limited power for subgroup event rates.

\section{Discussion}

We find that debiasing on retrospective ICU data reduces overall error and can reduce worst-group disparities; however, external validation on ENCoDE reveals that race-based gains do not uniformly translate to measured skin tone improvements. This supports the need for measured pigmentation in fairness evaluation, and suggests that race-ethnicity proxies may be insufficient for clinical deployment claims. The tradeoff between overall MAE and hidden hypoxemia highlights the importance of explicitly prioritizing clinically important errors.

We interpret the partial transfer of race-based gains as consistent with cohort and measurement differences: ENCoDE includes multiple devices and anatomical locations, and the device/location sensitivity analyses show non-trivial variation in MAE. In addition, ENCoDE subgroup sizes and event counts are smaller than BOLD (Table~\ref{tab:group-counts}), increasing uncertainty and making some fairness estimates unstable. These factors emphasize that fairness conclusions are sensitive to device mix, measurement location, and cohort composition.

We draw three explicit takeaways for MLHC fairness practice: (1) validate debiasing claims on measured clinical attributes (e.g., skin tone) whenever available rather than relying solely on race/ethnicity, (2) report tradeoffs between aggregate error and clinically critical errors like hidden hypoxemia, and (3) accompany subgroup metrics with group counts and event rates to avoid over-interpreting unstable estimates.

\paragraph{Limitations}
ENCoDE has relatively low event rates for hidden hypoxemia, yielding wide bootstrap CIs and limited power for subgroup FNR/FPR estimates. Transfer models are constrained by feature overlap between datasets. We flag models as pathological when test-set FNR is $\geq 0.99$ and report them in the appendix; isotonic variants met this criterion in some splits and are excluded from the main results. Finally, we do not analyze downstream clinical outcomes; our focus is measurement bias rather than clinical decision impact.

\paragraph{Ethics and Data Use}
This study uses de-identified, publicly available datasets (BOLD and ENCoDE) accessed under their respective data use agreements. We report only aggregate results and do not attempt re-identification. No additional patient contact or intervention was performed.

\subsection{Isotonic Calibration Pathology}
In some splits, isotonic regression reduced MAE while substantially increasing hidden hypoxemia rates. We exclude these models from the main results and report them here to illustrate the importance of monitoring clinically meaningful error types alongside aggregate metrics.
